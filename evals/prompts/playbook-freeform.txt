# The 5 Rules of Getting People to Actually Change How They Work

70% of change initiatives fail. Not because the strategy is bad. Because nobody addressed the humans.

Why? Because organizations keep treating change as a strategy problem. Announce the new thing, run a training, update the process doc, send a Slack message, hope for the best. Then six months later, wonder why everyone's still doing things the old way.

Here's the thing: getting people to change how they work is not a strategy challenge. It's a human challenge. And there's decades of behavioral science telling us exactly how to do it well.

These are the 5 rules. They're simple. They're not easy.

## Rule 1: Start at the End

**Show the destination. Make people feel it.**

People can't move toward something they can't picture. And they won't move toward something they don't feel.

The Heath brothers nailed this in *Switch*: logic makes people think, emotion makes people act. You need both. A clear picture of where you're going, and an emotional reason to get there.

But here's what most managers do wrong. They say "we're rolling out a new process" and expect that to mean something. It doesn't. It's like telling your team "we're going on a trip" without saying where.

**What actually works:**

**Try it yourself first.** Before you ask anyone on your team to work differently, you need to have done it yourself. Not once. Repeatedly. You should be able to show the new way working, not just talk about it. If you're pushing a new tool, you'd better be using it daily. If you're restructuring the workflow, you'd better have tested it on your own work. Can't show a destination you haven't visited.

**Explain the why in local terms.** Not "the company decided" or "leadership wants us to." Your team doesn't work for the strategy deck. Tell them what this means for *their* work, specifically. "Here's how this changes our Tuesday client prep" beats "here's our transformation roadmap" every time.

**Make the destination concrete.** "By June, we'll be turning around client proposals in 2 days instead of 2 weeks." That's a destination. "We're optimizing our cross-functional workflows" is a press release.

**Use the Magic Question.** Here's a technique borrowed from solution-focused therapy that forces specificity. Ask your team: *"Imagine you went to sleep tonight, and during the night the change happened. You wake up and go to work without knowing it happened. What clues would you see and hear that would tell you the change had taken place?"* Vague destinations become concrete when people have to describe what they'd actually observe.

**Write a Definition of Done.** Once the destination is vivid, pin it down. Write a clear, testable statement of what success looks like, one the whole team can point to. "We'll know we're done when every client onboarding follows the new process end-to-end without reverting." The Magic Question opens the imagination. The Definition of Done closes it into commitment.

**Connect to something they care about.** Easier work? Better customer outcomes? Less weekend fire-fighting? Not getting left behind while the team across the hall ships twice as fast? Find the angle that resonates with *your* specific team. Not every team cares about the same thing.

## Rule 2: Make It Safe

**People won't try what they can't afford to fail at.**

Amy Edmondson's research is clear: psychological safety is the prerequisite for learning. Google's Project Aristotle confirmed it across 180+ teams: it's the **#1 factor in team effectiveness**. More than talent, structure, or resources.

Now add this: Edgar Schein identified **two types of anxiety** that operate during any change. **Survival anxiety**, the sense that if I don't change, something bad will happen (I'll fall behind, lose relevance, miss opportunities). And **learning anxiety**, the fear of trying something new: temporary incompetence, looking foolish, losing your identity as the expert, being excluded from your group, losing power or position.

Here's the key insight: **change begins only when survival anxiety exceeds learning anxiety.** Most managers instinctively try to increase survival anxiety: more urgency, more pressure, more warnings about falling behind. But the research says the better move is to **decrease learning anxiety.** Make the learning process less threatening, not the status quo more frightening.

If you skip this part, nothing else works. You'll roll out new processes, run workshops, set deadlines, and people will nod politely and keep doing things the old way.

**What actually works:**

**Go first and show your mistakes.** Share your own fumbling attempts with the new way of working. "I tried running last week's sprint review with the new format. It was clunky. Here's what I'm changing for next time." This gives your team permission to struggle. Research shows that a single leader demonstration can dramatically shift a team's willingness to try something new. Not eventually. Almost immediately.

**Acknowledge what's being lost.** The old way had real value. Say it out loud. "I know the old process felt faster. You were good at it. You'd built real expertise. This is going to be awkward for a while." Name it before you sell the benefits. If you skip the loss, people will protect what they're losing instead of reaching for what's next.

**Don't punish early failure.** When someone tries the new thing and it doesn't work, there's exactly one right response: "What did you learn?" Not "why didn't that work?" Not silence. Not a look. If a single stumble leads to a raised eyebrow in a meeting, you've just killed adoption for the next six months.

**Ask and actually listen.** In your 1:1s this week: "How's the new process going? What's hard about it?" Then shut up. Hear the answer. Don't defend the initiative, don't troubleshoot, don't reassure. Just listen.

**Train the group, not just the individual.** Resistance to change often lives in group norms. Send one person to training and they come back to a team that hasn't changed; the group pulls them back. When you train whole teams together, new norms can emerge. Schedule team learning sessions, not just individual courses.

**Create practice fields.** People need space to experiment without consequences. Set up sandbox time: a low-stakes pilot run, a practice week, a dry exercise where nobody's measuring output. When failure has zero organizational cost, people actually try things.

## Rule 3: Script the Steps

**Don't ask people to "embrace change." Tell them what to do Monday morning.**

Thaler and Sunstein showed that **defaults beat willpower** almost every time. The Heath brothers call it "scripting the critical moves": when you want someone to change, don't give them a direction. Give them a specific instruction.

"Start using the new system" is not an instruction. It's a wish.

**What actually works:**

**Give specific instructions.** "Tomorrow, log your first client call in the new tool instead of the old one. Just one call. That's it." That's something a person can do. "Explore how to integrate the new platform into your daily workflow" is something a person will put off forever.

**Find who's already doing it.** Someone on your team has figured something out. Maybe they've quietly started using the new process. Maybe they found a shortcut that makes the transition painless. Find that person. Ask them exactly what they do. Then help them show others. The Heath brothers call these "bright spots": the change is already happening somewhere. Your job is to find it and spread it.

**Remove friction.** When someone says "I would, but..." fix that "but." Access issue? Get them access today. No time? Carve out 30 minutes. Don't know how? Pair them with someone who does. Every "but" is a fixable blocker disguised as a permanent excuse.

**Make the new way the easy way.** Don't add the change on top of what people already do. Embed it into what they already do. Can the team's standard template include the new step? Can the meeting prep checklist default to the new format? When *not* doing the new thing requires a conscious decision, adoption takes care of itself.

## Rule 4: Start Small, to go Big

**Begin contained. Expand with proof.**

Karl Weick's research on small wins is one of the most underrated findings in organizational psychology. Small wins **"build a pattern that attracts allies and deters opponents."** They reduce stakes enough that failure doesn't derail everything. And they create momentum that's almost impossible to manufacture any other way.

Most managers get this wrong by going too broad, too fast. Five workstreams, three new tools, the whole department at once. That's not ambition. That's a recipe for mediocrity across the board.

**What actually works:**

**Pick one thing, not five.** One use case. One workflow. Maybe one team. Nail that before expanding. Resist the urge to boil the ocean. The fastest way to scale is to go small first.

**Start with people who want to try it.** Don't waste your energy converting skeptics. Find the two or three people who are already curious. Let them succeed. Their success will do more convincing than any all-hands presentation you could deliver.

**Set clear "expand when" criteria.** Before you start the pilot, define what success looks like. Not a vague feeling. Specific markers. "When the pilot team has used the new process for 3 weeks and reports it saves time, we'll expand to the next team." This protects you from both premature scaling and indefinite piloting.

**Protect the pilot from pressure to scale too fast.** When your VP asks "why aren't we doing this everywhere?" hold the line. A pilot that works is worth ten rollouts that fizzle. You're building a foundation, not performing.

**Plan a small wins ladder.** Don't just pick one win; plan a sequence of 3-6 wins, where each explicitly sets up the next. Win one: one team member uses the new process for one task. Win two: they show the rest of the team. Win three: the whole team tries it for one week. Not isolated experiments, a visible staircase where each step makes the next one obvious.

## Rule 5: Make Progress Visible

**Communicate relentlessly. Show wins. Sustain the narrative.**

Kotter's research found that most change efforts **under-communicate by a factor of 10x.** Managers think they've said it enough. They haven't. Not even close.

Progress that nobody sees doesn't build momentum. It doesn't convert skeptics. It doesn't sustain energy. It just quietly fades until someone asks "whatever happened with that new thing we were doing?"

**What actually works:**

**Share what's working.** When someone on your team figures something out, don't let it stay private. "Sarah found a great way to handle the new intake process; I asked her to show the team Thursday." That's it. That's the whole move. Find it, name it, share it.

**Talk about it regularly.** Not one announcement and done. Mention it in team meetings. Bring it up in 1:1s. Reference it in casual conversation. The change stays alive because you keep it visible, not because you made a great launch presentation.

**Connect progress to outcomes people care about.** "Adoption is at 60%" means nothing to your team. "We saved 12 hours last week" means everything. "We got the proposal out a day early" means even more. Translate progress into the language your team actually thinks in.

**Follow up.** This is the one everyone skips. If you said you'd find out the answer to someone's question, come back with the answer. If you asked someone to try something, ask how it went. Follow-through is how you signal that this actually matters, not just this week, but for real.
